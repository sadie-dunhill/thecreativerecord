### Hook Post 1

**Twitter/X:**
Bad hook: "Our protein powder is the best on the market."
Good hook: "I stopped drinking whey protein after I saw this."
The difference? Specificity and curiosity. One states. The other implies a story.
Your hook should make them NEED to know what comes next.

**LinkedIn:**
Bad hook: "Our protein powder is the best on the market."

Good hook: "I stopped drinking whey protein after I saw this."

The difference? Specificity and curiosity.

The bad hook states a claim. The good hook implies a story with stakes. It creates an information gap the viewer needs to close.

Most DTC brands write hooks like the first example. They lead with the product, the feature, the claim.

The brands winning at scale write hooks like the second. They lead with curiosity, transformation, or tension.

Your hook has 3 seconds. Don't waste them on claims your viewer has heard 100 times.

### Hook Post 2

**Twitter/X:**
Before: "Tired of bad sleep? Try our melatonin gummies."
After: "My doctor told me to stop taking melatonin. Here's what I do instead."
Authority + contradiction + curiosity. That's the formula. Stop describing the problem. Create tension around the solution.

**LinkedIn:**
Before: "Tired of bad sleep? Try our melatonin gummies."

After: "My doctor told me to stop taking melatonin. Here's what I do instead."

Same product. Completely different performance.

The first hook:
- States the obvious problem
- Presents the generic solution
- Blends in with every other sleep supplement ad

The second hook:
- Establishes authority ("my doctor")
- Creates contradiction (told to STOP taking the expected solution)
- Builds curiosity (what's the alternative?)

This is the "unique mechanism" angle at work. Instead of fighting for attention in the crowded "better sleep" category, it creates a new category: "the melatonin alternative."

The lesson: Don't describe the problem better than your competitors. Create tension around the solution.

### Hook Post 3

**Twitter/X:**
Weak: "Revolutionary skincare that actually works."
Strong: "I spent $3,000 on skincare before I found this $28 routine."
Numbers create credibility. Specificity beats superlatives. "Revolutionary" means nothing. "$3,000 vs $28" means everything.

**LinkedIn:**
Weak: "Revolutionary skincare that actually works."

Strong: "I spent $3,000 on skincare before I found this $28 routine."

The first hook uses a superlative ("revolutionary") that every skincare brand claims. It means nothing because it means everything.

The second hook uses specificity that creates instant credibility:
- The exact amount spent before ($3,000)
- The exact cost of the solution ($28)
- The implied transformation (from expensive failure to affordable success)

This is the "specificity" persuasion angle. Instead of claiming your product is better, you demonstrate it with concrete numbers and relatable experience.

The math: $3,000 ÷ $28 = 107x more expensive alternatives failed first.

That's a story. That's a hook. That's what stops the scroll.

Stop saying "revolutionary." Start saying specific.

### Hook Post 4

**Twitter/X:**
Before: "Get organized with our planner."
After: "I was failing every productivity system until I stopped trying to optimize my mornings."
Counter-positioning. The hook doesn't promise a better solution—it promises a different approach to the problem entirely.

**LinkedIn:**
Before: "Get organized with our planner."

After: "I was failing every productivity system until I stopped trying to optimize my mornings."

This is counter-positioning in action.

The first hook enters a crowded market with an undifferentiated message. It competes with every planner, app, and productivity tool on the shelf.

The second hook creates a new category by rejecting the dominant approach:
- Acknowledges the failure of existing solutions ("failing every productivity system")
- Identifies the hidden assumption ("optimizing mornings")
- Promises a different path (implied: there's a better way)

This is the "contrarian" angle. Instead of saying "our planner is better," it says "the whole productivity industry is focused on the wrong thing."

It attracts people who've tried and failed with existing solutions—which is most of the market.

Sometimes the best positioning is against the category, not within it.

### Hook Post 5

**Twitter/X:**
Flat: "Introducing our new sustainable water bottle."
Sticky: "I used 167 plastic water bottles last month. Then my roommate showed me this."
Story beats statement. The number is specific. The "roommate" is relatable. The transformation is implied, not claimed.

**LinkedIn:**
Flat: "Introducing our new sustainable water bottle."

Sticky: "I used 167 plastic water bottles last month. Then my roommate showed me this."

Three reasons the second hook wins:

1. **Specificity**: "167 plastic water bottles" is concrete. "Sustainable" is abstract. The number makes the problem feel real and measurable.

2. **Relatability**: "My roommate showed me" is a discovery story, not a sales pitch. It mirrors how people actually find products—through people they trust.

3. **Implied transformation**: The hook doesn't claim the product is better. It sets up a before/after narrative the viewer wants to complete.

This is the "transformation" angle applied to a hook. Instead of describing the product, it describes the journey from problem to solution.

The viewer doesn't just learn about a water bottle. They see themselves in the story.

That's what makes them stop scrolling.

### Script Breakdown 1

**Twitter/X:**
Script breakdown: Why this $50M skincare brand's ad works

Hook: "I stopped using retinol after this happened..."
Why it works: Creates open loop + stakes. Viewers NEED to know what happened.

Structure:
0-3s: Hook with personal story
3-8s: Problem (irritation, redness)
8-15s: Solution intro (alternative ingredient)
15-30s: Results + CTA

Every beat earns the next. No wasted seconds.

**LinkedIn:**
Script breakdown: Why this $50M skincare brand's ad works

**The Hook (0-3s):**
"I stopped using retinol after this happened..."

Why it works: Open loop + stakes. The viewer doesn't know what "this" is, but the speaker's tone implies it was bad. They need to close the information gap.

**Structure breakdown:**

0-3s: Hook with personal story (establishes credibility and curiosity)
3-8s: Problem dramatization (shows retinol irritation, builds empathy)
8-15s: Solution introduction (presents alternative without bashing retinol)
15-30s: Results + soft CTA (shows transformation, asks for engagement)

**Why this structure works:**

1. Every beat earns the next. The hook creates curiosity. The problem justifies the solution. The solution delivers on the promise.

2. No wasted seconds. At 30 seconds, you have no time for fluff. Every frame moves the narrative forward.

3. The CTA isn't "buy now." It's "comment if you want to know more." Lower friction, higher engagement, algorithm boost.

This is the "transformation" angle executed in 30 seconds.

Save this structure for your next video ad.

Why this pet food ad hit 3x ROAS:

Then: explains the "crime" (feeding cheap kibble)
Then: introduces solution (fresh food)

**LinkedIn:**
Why this pet food ad hit 3x ROAS:

**The Hook:**
"My vet fired me as a client."

Pattern interrupt in 6 words. Nobody expects a vet to "fire" a client. It creates instant curiosity and a thousand questions.

**The Structure:**

1. **The reveal (3-8s):** Explains the "crime"—feeding cheap kibble that was making the dog sick. The vet fired her because she wouldn't listen.

2. **The turn (8-15s):** Introduces fresh food as the solution. Not as a product pitch, but as "what finally made me listen."

3. **The transformation (15-30s):** The same vet who fired her now recommends this food to other clients. Full circle.

**Why it works:**

- **Enemy positioning:** The "bad" pet food industry vs. the enlightened owner
- **Credibility through transformation:** The vet's change of heart is more powerful than any product claim
- **Story arc:** The hook's promise (why fired?) pays off in the close (vet now recommends)

This is the "enemy" angle executed through narrative. The enemy isn't the vet—it's the cheap kibble industry that made the vet necessary.

The lesson: Your hook's promise must pay off in your close. Otherwise it's just clickbait.

**LinkedIn:**
Why this hair care ad stops the scroll:

**The Hook:**
"Stop putting coconut oil in your hair."

Six words that create immediate tension. Coconut oil is a sacred cow in hair care. Challenging it triggers defensive curiosity.

**The Formula:**
Challenge → Explain → Replace → Prove

**Challenge (0-3s):** "Stop putting coconut oil in your hair."

**Explain (3-10s):** Why it doesn't work—molecules too big to penetrate, sits on surface, weighs hair down. Science-lite, accessible, credible.

**Replace (10-18s):** Their product with smaller molecular structure. Not just different—better for a specific, explained reason.

**Prove (18-30s):** 30-day results. Before/after that's dramatic but believable.

**Why it works:**

- **Contrarian angle:** Challenges accepted wisdom
- **Education as persuasion:** Doesn't just claim superiority, explains it
- **Clear alternative:** Once they dismantle coconut oil, their solution is the obvious choice

This is the "unique mechanism" approach. The product isn't just better—it's different in a way that matters.

The lesson: Sometimes the best hook is telling people to stop doing what they're already doing.

### Script Breakdown 5

**Twitter/X:**
This fitness app's ad uses a classic 3-act structure in 30 seconds:

Act 1: "I tried every workout program. Nothing stuck."
(Relatability—establishes the attempt)

Act 2: "Then I found [app]. It was different because..."
(Differentiation—explains the mechanism)

Act 3: "180 days later, I'm in the best shape of my life."
(Transformation—delivers the promise)

Simple. Effective. Timeless.

**LinkedIn:**
This fitness app's ad uses a classic 3-act structure in 30 seconds:

**Act 1: The Setup (0-10s)**
"I tried every workout program. P90X. CrossFit. OrangeTheory. Nothing stuck."

Relatability through specificity. Naming specific programs shows real attempt, not lazy dismissal.

**Act 2: The Turn (10-20s)**
"Then I found [app]. It was different because it adapted to my schedule, not the other way around."

Differentiation through mechanism. Doesn't claim "better workouts." Explains WHY it works for people who've failed before.

**Act 3: The Transformation (20-30s)**
"180 days later, I'm in the best shape of my life. And I only work out 20 minutes a day."

Transformation that addresses the core objection (time). The number is specific. The result is clear.

**Why this structure works:**

1. **Pattern recognition:** We've seen this story structure in movies, books, and myths. It feels complete.

2. **Promise and payoff:** The opening's problem (nothing sticks) is resolved by the close (best shape of my life).

3. **Objection handling:** "180 days" acknowledges transformation takes time. "20 minutes" addresses the time constraint.

This is the "transformation" narrative in its purest form. Before → Discovery → After.

The lesson: Classic story structure works because humans are wired for it. Don't reinvent—execute.

### Tip Post 1

**Twitter/X:**
Quick win for your video ads:

Add a 1-second black frame before your hook.

Why: It signals "new content" to the viewer's brain. Creates a micro-moment of attention reset.

Sounds stupid. Tested it across 12 accounts. Average 8% improvement in thumb-stop rate.

Small hinge, big door.

**LinkedIn:**
Quick win for your video ads:

Add a 1-second black frame before your hook.

**Why this works:**

Social feeds are continuous streams of stimulation. Viewers' brains are in passive scroll mode. A black frame creates a micro-moment of disruption—a visual "reset button" that signals new content is starting.

**The results:**
Tested across 12 ad accounts in Q1 2024. Average 8% improvement in thumb-stop rate (some as high as 15%). Cost per thumb stop dropped proportionally.

**Implementation:**
- Add 1 second of black before your hook
- No audio during the black frame
- Let the hook audio start with the visual

**Why most people won't do this:**
It feels wrong. Creative teams hate black frames. They want branding, motion, energy from frame one.

But frame one isn't for you. It's for the viewer's brain.

Small hinge, big door.

vs.

Delete the question marks. Make statements.

**LinkedIn:**
The 3-second rule for video ads:

If the viewer doesn't understand your premise in 3 seconds, you've lost them.

**Not:** "If they're intrigued."
**Not:** "If they want to learn more."

They need to understand what's happening and why they should care.

**The clarity test:**
Show your ad to someone who doesn't know your product. Mute the audio. Watch for 3 seconds. Ask them:

1. What's happening?
2. Why should I care?
3. What's the promise?

If they can't answer all three, your hook isn't clear enough.

**Why this matters:**
The algorithm tracks thumb-stop rate. If people scroll past in 2 seconds, your ad dies. If they watch for 5+ seconds, it gets distribution.

Clarity beats cleverness. Always.

The best hooks aren't the most creative. They're the most comprehensible.

Test for clarity before you test for creativity.
