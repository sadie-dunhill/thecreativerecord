# The Creative Performance Analysis
## How Goodo Studios Reads Ad Data to Make Better Creative (Not More Expensive Media Buys)

**Version 2.0** | The Creative Record | March 2026

---

## Introduction: Why Most Creative Analysis Gets It Wrong

Most agencies analyze creative performance and produce reports that look like media buying recommendations.

"Scale winning ad sets." "Increase budget on top performers." "Retarget with bottom-of-funnel copy." These recommendations might be accurate, but they're not creative strategy. They're media strategy. And creative agencies who produce media strategy are doing two things at once and doing neither particularly well.

The Goodo deep analysis does one thing: tells you what creative is working, why it's working, what to make next, and what iteration type will produce the strongest result.

Not "scale this ad set." "This UGC format with a Contrarian Take hook is outperforming every other format by 2.5x. Here's the specific formula. Make three more following this pattern. Here's the brief."

That distinction -- creative diagnosis vs. media recommendation -- is what makes the analysis actionable for the people who produce content rather than the people who allocate budget.

This document is the complete Goodo creative performance analysis system: the pre-analysis rules, the 10-section report structure, the iteration playbook, and the failure diagnosis framework that tells you exactly why a creative isn't working.

## The Pre-Analysis Rules

These aren't preferences. They're structural constraints that, if violated, produce analyses that lead to wrong decisions.

### Rule 1: Sort by Spend, Not ROAS

ROAS without spend context is noise.

A creative with 8x ROAS on $200 spend is almost certainly noise -- a small audience, a lucky week, or a campaign configuration issue. The algorithm hasn't had enough budget to tell you anything.

A creative with 2.4x ROAS on $15,000 spend is a signal -- the algorithm has spent real money behind it, which means it's performing well enough to earn continued investment.

**The principle:** Meta puts money behind what works. High spend at reasonable ROAS is a stronger signal than high ROAS at low spend. Sort the creative list by spend before you do anything else. Your top 5 are the top 5 by spend. The ROAS tells you how efficiently each one is working, not whether it's working.

---

### Rule 2: Separate Goodo Creatives from All Other Creatives

Every account has two creative populations: what Goodo made and what everyone else made (client repurposed content, previous agency work, in-house).

These populations need separate evaluation.

The question "Is Goodo's work moving the needle?" can only be answered if Goodo's work is isolated from the pool. A single analysis that mixes all creative tells you what's working in the account but not whether your specific contribution is driving that performance.

Goodo creatives get a separate section. They're evaluated on: their share of spend, their ROAS relative to account benchmark, their hook rates relative to non-Goodo creative.

The honest version of this evaluation is: Are we the agency this client should be working with based on what the data shows?

---

### Rule 3: No Competitor Analysis

The analysis covers this client's data only. Not competitor ads, not category benchmarks, not "here's what the best brands in your space are doing."

There are two reasons for this:
1. You don't have performance data for competitor creative. You have public-facing creative. You have no idea whether their ads are working.
2. Optimizing against competitors is a distraction. The question is "what does THIS account need?" not "what is that account doing?"

---

### Rule 4: No Budget or Media Buying Suggestions

Goodo makes creative. Not media plans.

This is a hard boundary, not a soft preference. The moment a creative analysis starts recommending budget allocations, campaign structures, or targeting changes, you've crossed from creative diagnosis into media buying. These are different disciplines. Mixing them produces analysis that neither the creative team nor the media team can fully act on.

**What the analysis says:** "This format is driving 2.5x the ROAS of other formats. Make more of it."
**What the analysis does NOT say:** "Allocate 40% of budget to this campaign and scale to $500/day."

If a media strategy change is clearly implied by the creative data, note it as an observation: "Data suggests the cold audience creative mix may be underserving awareness-stage prospects. A creative strategist and a media buyer should discuss this together." Then stop.

---

### Rule 5: Every Recommendation Cites Specific Data

"Test UGC" is not a recommendation. It's a category.

"Travel TSA UGC (0.802 ROAS, 49.9% TSR) is outperforming every other format by 2.5x at $8,400 spend. Produce 3 more UGC concepts targeting travel-adjacent audiences following this hook formula: Specific Person + Problem, beginning with the security line scenario. The formula: [exact hook written out]" -- that's a recommendation.

Every item in the Production Action Plan must cite: the creative it's based on, the metric that supports it, the specific production direction that follows from it.

Untethered recommendations -- "try more social proof" -- suggest you read the brief and wrote the report without actually reading the data.

---

### Rule 6: Client-Specific Benchmarks Only

Don't use industry averages for benchmark comparisons.

The account you're analyzing has its own history. What's a "good" hook rate for this brand might be meaningless against a category average that includes brands with completely different creative strategies, audiences, and products.

Build benchmarks from the account's own non-promotional data:
- Non-Black Friday, non-holiday-sale periods
- Excluding launch weeks for new products
- Rolling 60-day performance windows

A creative that's "below industry average" but 20% above this account's own baseline is performing well. A creative that "matches industry average" but is 30% below this account's baseline is underperforming. Client-specific benchmarks tell you which is which.

## The 10-Section Report Structure

### Section 1: Executive Summary

**Length:** 3-4 sentences maximum.

**What it contains:**
- The single most important finding from this analysis (not a list -- the ONE finding)
- The pattern to double down on
- What to produce next, specific enough to action immediately
- Three highest-leverage creative moves

**What it does not contain:**
- Background on the brand
- Account history
- Context for the context
- Anything that could appear in a standard agency report for any client

**The test:** Can the strategist who reads this executive summary and nothing else walk into a production meeting with a clear agenda? If yes, it's written correctly. If they'd need to read the rest of the report first, the executive summary is just a table of contents.

---

### Section 2: Account Health Snapshot

**Format:** Tables. Not narrative.

**What to include:**
- Period comparison table (week-over-week or year-over-year -- choose the timeframe that tells the most honest story)
- Current metrics vs. account baseline
- WoW/YoY trend: improving, flat, or declining (with the actual percentage, not just the label)
- Any anomaly that context explains (Black Friday spike, a single day of high spend, etc.)

**What to avoid:**
- 365-day rolling averages. They include Black Friday, holiday promotions, and any single campaign that skewed the account. Rolling averages make good accounts look mediocre and bad weeks look acceptable.
- Narrative explanations for data when the data speaks clearly
- "The account is performing well overall" -- the table says this better than the sentence

---

### Section 3: Goodo Creative Performance

**Purpose:** Separate, honest evaluation of work Goodo produced.

**What to include:**
- Goodo share of spend (what % of total account spend went to Goodo-produced creative?)
- Goodo ROAS vs. account average
- Goodo hook rate vs. non-Goodo
- Top 3 Goodo creatives by spend (with why they're working)
- Bottom 3 Goodo creatives by performance (with why they're not)

**The honest question this section answers:** Is Goodo's work moving the needle? If no: why not, and what changes in the brief or production process would address it?

This section is not for client-facing reporting. It's internal. It keeps Goodo honest about whether the work is actually performing.

---

### Section 4: Top 5 Creatives (by Spend)

For each of the 5 highest-spend creatives:

**Metrics table:**
| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Spend | $X |
| ROAS | X.Xx |
| Hook Rate (TSR) | XX% |
| Hold Rate | XX% |
| CPM | $X |
| CTR | X.X% |

**The hook analysis (0-3 seconds):**
- Visual hook: exact description of what viewer sees
- Audio hook: exact first words spoken
- Text hook: exact on-screen text
- Hook formula identified (which of 7 formulas does this use?)
- Positioning angle identified (which of 8 angles is this built on?)
- Awareness stage: what stage does this creative target?

**Why Meta is spending there:**
Not "good performance." The specific signal in the metrics that explains algorithm behavior. "TSR of 41% far above account average (26%) means the hook is stopping scroll at above-average rates. CPM is elevated, suggesting competitive placement -- Meta is bidding this up because it performs."

**Fatigue check:**
- Current frequency (how many times has the average user seen this?)
- ROAS trend over the past 3 weeks (improving, stable, declining)
- CPM trend over the past 3 weeks
- Verdict: Fresh / Tiring / Fatigued

**What to learn:**
The single most transferable insight from this creative. What formula, angle, structure, or specific execution can be extracted and applied to new creative?

**Iteration recommendation:**
One of the named iteration types from the playbook (see Section 10). Not "update the hook." "Hook Headline Swap -- keep the visual and audio, test 3 new text overlay variations targeting Problem-Aware vs. Solution-Aware positioning."

---

### Section 5: Weakest Performers at Scale

**Definition:** Creatives with significant spend AND poor ROAS. Not low-spend tests -- money was committed, performance is poor.

**Creative diagnosis framework:**
The diagnosis must identify the failure at the right level of the funnel:

| Signal | What It Means | Creative Diagnosis |
|--------|--------------|-------------------|
| Low TSR (<15%) | Hook didn't stop scroll | Hook failure -- visual, audio, or text failing to pattern-interrupt |
| High TSR + Low Hold (TSR 25%+, Hold Rate <10%) | Hook worked, body lost them | Engagement failure -- the setup didn't deliver on the hook's promise |
| Good TSR + Hold + Low ROAS | Entertains, doesn't sell | Conversion failure -- creative is content, not an ad |
| High ATC + Low Purchase | Promise doesn't match landing page | Funnel disconnect -- ad and landing page are misaligned |

**Why this precision matters:**

Prescribing "a better hook" to a Hold Rate failure wastes production. The hook already worked -- that's what the TSR is telling you. The problem is in the body. Rewriting the hook produces a brief that's right about the symptom but wrong about the cause.

Read the failure signal. Diagnose at the right level. Prescribe accordingly.

**For each weak performer:**
- Metrics (same table as Section 4)
- Failure signal identified
- Diagnosis at the funnel level
- Specific cause (not "weak copy" -- the specific structural or strategic failure)
- Revision recommendation (named iteration type + specific direction)

---

### Section 6: Format + Angle Analysis

**Purpose:** Identify which creative types the algorithm trusts for this account.

**Format analysis table:**

| Format | Spend | ROAS | Count | Notes |
|--------|-------|------|-------|-------|
| UGC -- Talking Head | $X | X.Xx | X | |
| Video -- Voiceover | $X | X.Xx | X | |
| Static -- Single Image | $X | X.Xx | X | |
| Video -- Testimonial | $X | X.Xx | X | |
| Carousel | $X | X.Xx | X | |

**Angle analysis table:**

| Angle | Spend | ROAS | Count | Notes |
|-------|-------|------|-------|-------|
| Contrarian | $X | X.Xx | X | |
| Unique Mechanism | $X | X.Xx | X | |
| Transformation | $X | X.Xx | X | |
| Social Proof | $X | X.Xx | X | |

**The three format questions:**
1. Which format does the algorithm trust most (highest spend at reasonable ROAS)?
2. Which format has strong ROAS but low volume (the "we should make more of this" signal)?
3. Which awareness stages are under-covered? If all high-spend creative is Solution-Aware or Product-Aware, there may be no cold audience creative working -- a structural gap.

---

### Section 7: Product/Category Performance

**Purpose:** Creative coverage analysis by product line.

**Product performance table:**

| Product | Spend | ROAS | CPA | Purchases | Creative Count |
|---------|-------|------|-----|-----------|----------------|

**The questions this section answers:**
- Which products have the most creative coverage?
- Which products are driving the most revenue relative to creative investment?
- Which products have strong performance but low creative count (need more coverage)?
- Which products have heavy creative investment but weak performance (creative angle problem or product problem)?

---

### Section 8: Strategic Briefs (2 Briefs)

The analysis doesn't end with observations. It ends with production-ready briefs.

**Brief 1: Build on What's Working**
This brief iterates on the top spend pattern. It uses the exact formula, angle, and structure that's producing the strongest results and builds a new execution. Not "make another UGC" -- "make another UGC using the Relatable Friend archetype with a Contrarian Take hook in the first 3 seconds, targeting the sleep dysregulation problem-awareness stage, because this combination is producing 2.4x the account ROAS average at the second-highest spend position."

**Brief 2: Fill the Biggest Gap**
This brief addresses the most significant underserved segment, format, or awareness stage. It's the creative risk brief -- the angle nobody has tested yet that the data suggests might work, or the format that's doing well in small tests and deserves more production investment.

Both briefs use the full daily creative brief format (see The Daily Creative Brief System skill).

---

### Section 9: Production Action Plan

**Maximum 5 items.** Not a wishlist -- the 5 things that will most directly impact performance.

Each item includes:
- What to make (specific, actionable)
- Why (cite the data: creative name, metric, number)
- Priority level (P1: this week / P2: this month / P3: backlog)

**What is explicitly NOT in this section:**
- Budget recommendations
- Campaign structure changes
- Targeting recommendations
- Audience segmentation advice
- Any suggestion that begins "Have your media buyer..."

If a production decision and a media decision are intertwined, note the media component separately as "coordinate with media buyer" -- but don't make the media decision in this document.

---

### Section 10: Data Gaps and Confidence Level

Intellectual honesty. This section documents what you don't know.

**Types of gaps:**
- Limited spend data (tests haven't had enough budget to produce reliable signals)
- Missing creative segments (no data on a format that could theoretically work)
- Attribution uncertainty (iOS changes or UTM gaps making ROAS calculations unreliable)
- Anomaly periods in the data window (sale events, promotional periods)

**Confidence rating for each major recommendation:**
- **High confidence:** Multiple creatives with significant spend all pointing the same direction
- **Directional signal:** 1-2 creatives with some spend suggesting a pattern worth testing
- **Hypothesis only:** Structural logic suggests this, but no data supports it yet

Including confidence levels prevents the analysis from presenting directional signals as confirmed findings. It also tells the production team what level of creative investment is warranted for each recommendation.

## The Iteration Playbook

When a creative needs refreshing or expanding, use the right type. Not "test a new hook" -- the specific named iteration type.

### Video Iteration Types

**Hook Headlines**
Keep everything after the first 3 seconds. Test multiple text hook variations. Cheapest possible iteration -- no reshoots.
*Use when:* Decent hold rate but low TSR. The body works; the hook isn't stopping scroll.

**Hook Visual Swap**
Keep the audio and copy. Change the visual first 3 seconds.
*Use when:* Low TSR on a strong body. The concept is right; the visual isn't pattern-interrupting.

**Comment Bubble Hook**
Add a "responding to comment" format to an existing video. Screenshot of a question, respond with the existing video content.
*Use when:* Existing video performs well but awareness stage is wrong for cold audiences. Comment bubble makes it feel native and creates curiosity before the product enters.

**Hook Stacking**
Create multiple 3-second hooks, each leading into the same middle and end.
*Use when:* Creative has high hold rate (body is strong) but TSR varies. Test different hooks against the same proven body to find the highest-stopping version.

**Length Trim**
Cut a longer video down to the shortest version that still delivers the complete sell.
*Use when:* Hold rate drops significantly after a specific timestamp. Trim at the drop point.

**Problem Agitation**
Add 5-10 seconds of problem agitation before the current body.
*Use when:* Good conversion rate among people who do watch, but low reach/spend. The concept is right; it needs more setup for cold audiences.

**Stack Winning Ads**
Combine the hook from one top performer with the body from another.
*Use when:* Two creatives with distinct strengths (one high TSR, one high hold rate) that haven't been combined.

### Creator/UGC Iteration Types

**Creator Reshoot (Same Script)**
Same script, different creator. Keeps the proven copy while testing creator archetype fit.
*Use when:* Conversion rate good but audience is fatiguing. New face, same message.

**Same Creator New Script**
Same creator (who tested well), new messaging angle.
*Use when:* Creator performed well but the script has plateaued. Audience is still warm to the creator.

**Gender Swap Creator**
Same script or concept, different gender presentation.
*Use when:* Data shows performance variation by gender in the audience. Test whether the same message lands differently.

**Greenscreen React**
Creator reacts to news, data, or content with greenscreen background.
*Use when:* Top-of-funnel creative is weak. Greenscreen react formats feel native and can work for Unaware or Problem-Aware audiences.

**Duet React**
Creator reacts to another piece of content (customer testimonial, competitor ad, news clip).
*Use when:* Social proof is strong in the brand doc. Creator endorsing an existing testimonial feels more authentic than reciting it.

**AI Avatar Swap**
Replace creator visuals with AI avatar, keep audio.
*Use when:* Testing whether the creator's appearance is adding to or detracting from performance.

### Messaging Iteration Types

**New Messaging Angle**
Same format, completely different positioning angle (from the 8 angles).
*Use when:* Format is proven for the account but the current angle has plateaued.

**Add Social Proof**
Layer in specific numbers, testimonials, or endorsements to existing creative.
*Use when:* Good TSR and hold rate but lower-than-expected conversion. The audience is watching but not buying. Social proof addresses the final objection.

**Awareness Level Shift**
Take a creative built for one awareness stage and adapt it for an adjacent stage.
*Use when:* Strong performance in one funnel segment, gaps in adjacent segments.

### Edit Iteration Types

**Split Screen**
Add side-by-side before/after or comparison format.
*Use when:* Transformation angle is strong in the brand doc but the current creative isn't visually demonstrating the contrast.

**B-Roll Refresh**
Keep audio; replace all B-roll footage with new imagery.
*Use when:* TSR and hold rate declining (audience has seen the footage). The message still works; the visual is fatiguing.

**Caption Style Variation**
Change caption font, size, color, or animation style.
*Use when:* TSR declining but hook concept is still strong. Visual fresh-up at minimal production cost.

**Music/SFX Swap**
Replace background music or sound effects while keeping all other elements.
*Use when:* Fatiguing creative where the music association may be triggering ad blindness.

**Multi-Ad Mashup**
Create new creative from segments of multiple existing ads.
*Use when:* Multiple creatives with strong specific sections (strong hook on one, strong proof section on another). Combine best of each.

### Format Swap Iteration Types

| From | To | When to Use |
|------|----|-------------|
| Video | Static | Strong message, weak visual execution. Test if static delivers the message more cleanly. |
| Video | Carousel | Multiple proof points competing for space. Carousel lets each breathe. |
| Video | Motion/GIF | Static performing well; motion might add without losing the format strength. |
| Static | Video | Strong headline and visual direction but losing to dynamic competition. |
| Static | Carousel | Multiple angles or products needing separate treatment. |
| Low Production | High Production | Concept proven; invest in production quality to extend creative life. |

### Static Iteration Types

**Headline Style Swap**
Test different headline formulas while keeping visual and CTA.
*Use when:* Visual is performing (low CPM) but CTR is weak.

**Social Proof Integration**
Add specific numbers, testimonials, or certifications to existing static.
*Use when:* Good reach, weak conversion. Cold audience sees it but doesn't trust it.

**CTA Variation**
Test different CTA copy, placement, or urgency framing.
*Use when:* High CTR but low purchase rate. Audience is clicking but landing page isn't converting -- or the CTA is creating wrong expectations.

## Key Benchmarks to Know

These are reference points, not absolute standards. Every account develops its own baseline.

**Hook Rate (Thumbstop Rate / TSR):**
- Target ≥25%
- 40%+ = excellent
- Below 15% = hook failure -- stop the campaign or replace the hook before more spend

**Hold Rate:**
- Target 15-20%+
- Strong hold rate with weak ROAS = conversion failure (the content entertains but doesn't sell)
- Weak hold rate with strong ROAS = a small but highly qualified audience completing the CTA

**CTR:**
- 1-3% generally good
- Context-dependent by placement and format

**Creative Fatigue Signals:**
- Frequency above 3x (average user has seen it 3 times)
- ROAS declining 3 weeks in a row
- CPM rising 3 weeks in a row

**Rule:** Refresh, don't kill. Fatiguing creative still has signal value. Refresh the hook or swap the format before pulling the plug.

## Implementation Guide: Running Your First Deep Analysis

### Pre-Work (Day Before)

Pull data from the ad account. Export:
- All active creatives with spend, ROAS, TSR, hold rate, CPM, CTR for the analysis period
- Same metrics for the comparison period (for WoW/YoY comparison)
- Product-level performance data if available

Sort by spend.

Identify top 5 and weakest 5 at scale. Note which are Goodo-produced vs. other.

### Analysis Session (Allow 2-3 Hours)

Work through sections in order, but write the executive summary last. The summary should reflect what you learned in the analysis, not what you expected to find going in.

Don't write from memory. Keep the data open. Every claim should be directly traceable to a number in the export.

Write the briefs (Section 8) before the Production Action Plan (Section 9). The briefs inform the action plan -- you'll know which 5 items matter most after you've written the briefs.

### Review Before Delivery

Before the report goes anywhere, run the following checks:

- [ ] Every recommendation cites specific creative + specific metric
- [ ] No budget or media buying suggestions
- [ ] No competitor analysis
- [ ] Top 5 sorted by spend (not ROAS)
- [ ] Goodo creative evaluated separately
- [ ] Each weak performer diagnosed at the funnel level (hook/engagement/conversion/funnel disconnect)
- [ ] Iteration types named specifically (not "try a new hook")
- [ ] Confidence levels noted for each major recommendation
- [ ] Data gaps documented honestly
- [ ] Production Action Plan has 5 items maximum

## QA Self-Assessment Checklist

### Pre-Analysis Compliance
- [ ] Sorted by spend
- [ ] Goodo creatives separated
- [ ] No competitor data included
- [ ] No budget recommendations
- [ ] Period comparison uses account-specific baseline (not industry average)
- [ ] Analysis window excludes anomaly periods (Black Friday, sales, launches)

### Section Quality
- [ ] Executive summary: 3-4 sentences, single most important finding
- [ ] Top 5: Hook analysis for each (visual + audio + text)
- [ ] Top 5: Fatigue check for each (frequency, trend, verdict)
- [ ] Weak performers: Diagnosed at funnel level (not just "weak creative")
- [ ] Format/Angle tables complete
- [ ] Product performance table complete

### Recommendations
- [ ] Every recommendation cites creative + metric + number
- [ ] Both strategic briefs use full brief format
- [ ] Production Action Plan: max 5 items
- [ ] Each iteration recommendation uses named type from playbook
- [ ] Confidence levels noted

### Honesty
- [ ] Data gaps documented
- [ ] Anomaly periods flagged
- [ ] No inflated confidence on directional signals
- [ ] Goodo section honest about performance, not promotional

If any box is unchecked: fix before delivery. The value of this analysis is its honesty and its specificity. Generic observations with vague recommendations are the most expensive possible use of a creative strategist's time.
