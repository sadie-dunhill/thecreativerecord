## Introduction: Why Most Briefs Fail Before They Ship

Most creative briefs fail at the same moment: when the person reading them realizes they have no idea what to actually shoot.

"Relatable creator" is not a creator. "Product shot" is not visual direction. "Attention-grabbing opening" is not a hook. These are placeholders dressed up as briefs -- they feel like work because they fill space, but they give a creative team nothing to execute against.

At Goodo Studios, we write briefs daily for active DTC clients. Not monthly strategy decks. Not quarterly creative refreshes. Daily. Each brief goes through a two-agent QA process that explicitly scores for specificity, brand doc integration, and hook quality before anything gets greenlit for production.

The result: a brief system that produces work where the creative team doesn't need to ask a single follow-up question.

This document is that system. Not a template. A system -- with the hard gates, the reference load order, the concept structure, the QA rubric, and the 19-mistake catalog that catches failures before they become expensive.

If you work in creative strategy for DTC brands -- or you want to -- this is how to write briefs that are actually usable.

## The Core Principle: Brand Doc as Hard Gate

The most important rule in the Goodo brief system isn't about format or length. It's about what has to exist before you write a single word.

**You cannot write a brief without verified, specific brand intelligence.**

This sounds obvious. It rarely gets enforced.

The brief system treats brand intelligence verification as a hard gate: a checkpoint that must pass before the brief proceeds. If the gate fails, the brief is flagged with `⚠️ BRAND DOC GAP` and the QA layer will catch it.

The 5 required elements before brief writing begins:

**1. At least one specific customer persona**
Not a demographic. A person. Age range, context, what they're doing when the ad reaches them. "Women 25-45 interested in wellness" is a demographic. "Woman, 34-38, scrolling Instagram on her lunch break, already bought two weighted blankets that ended up in the closet" is a persona.

**2. At least 3 specific hesitations to buy**
Not "price concerns" or "effectiveness doubts." Those are category placeholders. Real hesitations are specific: "I already tried something like this and it didn't work," "I don't know if this is safe for my medication," "I can't justify $200 on a blanket." Real objections have specificity you could write dialogue around.

**3. At least one proof point**
A stat, a quote, a measurable result. Not "customers love it." "93% of customers report falling asleep faster in the first week" is a proof point. "Thousands of happy customers" is a placeholder.

**4. The product mechanism**
How it actually works -- not just what it claims. "The weighted fill distributes pressure across sensory receptors, triggering the parasympathetic nervous system response" is a mechanism. "It helps you sleep" is a claim.

**5. What the product is NOT**
Misconceptions, claims to avoid, comparisons that make you look bad. This prevents the brief from accidentally writing ads that undermine the brand's positioning or invite the wrong comparison.

If you're missing any of these, stop. Go get them. A brief written without this information is not a brief -- it's a guess in brief-shaped clothing.

## The Reference Load Order

Once brand intelligence is verified, you load references in a specific order. The order matters because each subsequent document builds on the last.

**Load 1:** `clients/[CLIENT_SLUG]-brand-intelligence.md`
This is your primary source. Everything in the brief must trace back here. If a claim can't be sourced to the brand doc, it doesn't belong in the brief.

**Load 2:** `reference/strategy/advertising-frameworks.md`
Schwartz's 5 stages of awareness, Zaltman's 7 deep metaphors. You need these to determine what each concept needs to accomplish and which emotional register it lives in.

**Load 3:** `reference/strategy/direct-response-frameworks.md`
The 7 master hook formulas. Every hook in the brief must use a named formula from this document.

**Load 4:** `reference/strategy/positioning-angles.md`
The 8 positioning angles. Every concept must be built on a named angle, and you have to pass the Angle Test before finalizing.

**Load 5:** `reference/formats/video-ads-guide.md`
Format specifications, pacing structures, the Slippery Slide principle, bucket brigades. Only loaded for Concept 1 (Video).

**Load 6:** `reference/formats/creator-ugc-guide.md`
The 4 presenter archetypes, the 6 conversational frameworks, the authenticity paradox. Only loaded for Concept 2 (UGC/Creator).

**Load 7:** `reference/formats/static-ads-guide.md`
The 7 headline patterns, visual direction requirements, the One-Second Test. Only loaded for Concept 3 (Static).

This sequential loading isn't bureaucracy. It's how you get from "I know this brand" to "I know which of 7 emotional registers this ad should live in, which of 5 awareness stages this audience is at, which of 8 angles this hook is built on, which formula the hook uses, and which format structure serves all of that."

## The System: Step-by-Step

### Step 1: Load and Verify Brand Intelligence

Read the full brand doc. As you read, explicitly check off the 5 required elements.

If any are missing: stop and flag. Don't invent context. Don't assume. The creative will fail if it's built on guessed audience intelligence -- it just fails expensively at production stage instead of at the brief stage.

If all 5 are present: proceed.

**What to note while reading the brand doc:**
- Which hesitations appear most frequently in reviews? Those are your best objections to handle.
- What does the customer say in their own words? That language belongs in the brief -- not your marketing translation of it.
- Which proof points are most specific? Specific numbers beat general statements every time.
- What are the 2-3 things this brand does that no competitor can claim? Those are your positioning opportunities.

### Step 2: Identify Concept Distribution

Every brief has exactly 3 concepts. The awareness stage distribution is fixed:

- **Concept 1: Video Ad** → Cold audience (Unaware or Problem-Aware)
- **Concept 2: Creator/UGC** → Warm audience (Solution-Aware)
- **Concept 3: Static Ad** → Hot audience (Product-Aware or Most-Aware)

This distribution covers the full funnel in a single brief. The client can deploy all three knowing they'll reach different segments of their potential audience without message collision.

The brief must justify each awareness stage choice with reference to the brand doc. Not "this is a cold audience" -- "this is Unaware because the brand doc shows that most new customers didn't know they had a sleep regulation problem before discovering the product."

### Step 3: Load Strategy References

Read advertising frameworks with the brand doc fresh in your mind. For each of the 7 Zaltman deep metaphors, ask: which one does this product primarily live in? Which ones are secondary?

For Bearaby (weighted blanket for sleep): Primary = Balance (restoring equilibrium to a dysregulated sleep system). Secondary = Container (protecting sleep from external disruption) and Control (taking ownership of sleep instead of letting it happen passively).

The deep metaphor determines the emotional register of the brief. An ad that lives in Balance sounds and feels different from one that lives in Control. Same product, same facts -- completely different creative.

### Step 4: Determine Angles

Before writing any hooks, identify which of the 8 positioning angles each concept will use. Then run the Angle Test:

1. Is it true? (Can you back this up?)
2. Is it relevant? (Does this matter to your specific audience?)
3. Is it different? (Is this genuinely distinct from competitors?)
4. Is it provable? (Can you show evidence, not just claim it?)
5. Is it ownable? (Is this angle harder for competitors to copy?)

Score 4/5: Strong angle. Score 3/5: Refine before proceeding. Score below 3/5: Find a different angle.

Don't write the hook until the angle passes.

### Step 5: Write the Three Concepts

Use the exact output format below. No additions, no removals, no reordering.

For each concept:

**Write the hook first.** Not the structure, not the persuasion mechanism -- the hook. The hook is where the ad lives or dies. Get it right before building anything else around it.

A hook is three things simultaneously:
- **Visual:** What the viewer literally sees in the first 3 seconds
- **Audio:** The exact words spoken or sounds heard
- **Text overlay:** The on-screen text that delivers the message to viewers with sound off

All three must be present. Text hook is non-negotiable: 85% of viewers scroll with sound off.

**Then write the structure.** Beat-by-beat, with actual dialogue or exact visual descriptions. Not "show product benefits" -- "VO: 'By the second night, I wasn't waking up at 2am anymore.'"

**Then write the persuasion mechanism.** Name it. Explain why it applies to this specific audience at this specific awareness stage.

**Then write "Why This Works."** This section cites specific facts from the brand intelligence doc. It's your proof that this concept is grounded in real audience intelligence, not intuition.

### Step 6: Save and Flag

Save the brief at `creative/daily-strategy/[DATE]-[CLIENT_SLUG].md`.

If any brand doc gaps were identified at Step 1, note them at the top of the brief: `⚠️ BRAND DOC GAP: [specific missing element]`. The QA layer will catch this and the brief will be flagged for revision before production.

## Examples: Weak vs. Strong

The clearest way to see the system in action is to compare what it produces against what it replaces.

### Example 1: Creator/UGC Profile

**Weak (from a Pela Case brief without full brand doc integration):**
> Creator Profile: Style-focused creator, 25-35, filming their phone setup, aesthetic content.
>
> Opening line: "I never thought I'd care about my phone case. Then I found Pela."

Why this fails:
- "Style-focused creator" describes nobody. It's a vibe, not a person.
- "Filming their phone setup" is a setting, not a context.
- The opening line is a generic discovery narrative that could work for any phone case, any brand, any year.
- Nothing in this concept traces back to what the Pela brand doc says about its customers, their hesitations, or the specific environmental angle that makes Pela distinct.

**Strong (from a Bearaby brief with full brand doc integration):**
> Creator Profile: Woman, 34-38, filming in her living room on a weekend morning. Oversized sweatshirt, no makeup, hair up. Behind her: a clean-ish but lived-in couch, a floor lamp, some books stacked on a side table. No ring light -- just window light from the left. Energy: slightly exasperated but genuine, like she's telling a friend something she wishes she'd known sooner. Looks like a UX designer or marketing manager who actually does her research before buying anything.
>
> Opening line: "I'd tried three weighted blankets before this one, and they all ended up in the closet by March."

Why this works:
- The profile is specific enough that casting can find this person.
- The setting, wardrobe, and light source give the production team shot specs without a separate conversation.
- The opening line references a specific objection from the Bearaby brand doc ("I already tried a weighted blanket") and addresses it immediately in the first sentence.
- The energy description ("like she's telling a friend something she wishes she'd known sooner") tells the creator how to feel, not just what to say.

The difference isn't talent -- it's brand doc specificity.

### Example 2: Video Hook

**Weak:**
> Hook: An attention-grabbing opening that shows the contrast between poor sleep and good sleep. Someone looks exhausted, then rested.
>
> Formula: Before/After

Why this fails:
- "An attention-grabbing opening" is not a hook. It's a description of what a hook should feel like.
- "Someone looks exhausted, then rested" could be any sleep product made since 1990.
- There's no text hook for sound-off viewers.
- Nothing stops scroll -- there's no pattern interrupt, no specific moment that would make someone's thumb pause.

**Strong (actual Bearaby concept):**
> Formula: Contrarian Take
>
> Visual: Extreme close-up of bare feet kicking a heavy blanket off the bed at 2am.
>
> Audio: "Weighted blankets are supposed to help you sleep." (Voiceover, delivered flatly, after blanket hits floor.)
>
> Text overlay: "Why do I keep overheating?"

Why this works:
- The visual is exact -- feet kicking, blanket hitting floor, 2am. A cinematographer can shoot this without asking a question.
- The audio creates an open loop: you said they're supposed to help you sleep, but I just kicked one off. Now what?
- The text hook delivers the punchline to sound-off viewers.
- It leads with a brand doc-sourced objection: "I already have a weighted blanket but I wake up overheating" is one of the top 3 hesitations documented for Bearaby prospects.
- The Contrarian Take formula is correctly applied: it challenges a belief the target audience holds ("weighted blankets help sleep") using their own experience as evidence.

### Example 3: "Why This Works" Section

**Weak:**
> Why This Works: This concept will resonate with health-conscious consumers who are looking for better sleep solutions. The authentic UGC format builds trust and the personal story makes the product relatable.

Why this fails:
- "Health-conscious consumers looking for better sleep solutions" is a demographic, not an insight.
- "Builds trust" and "makes it relatable" are marketing truisms that could apply to any UGC for any product.
- Nothing cites the brand doc. This section is making claims that aren't sourced.
- A strategist reading this learns nothing they didn't already know.

**Strong:**
> Why This Works for Bearaby: The brand doc's hesitation list shows that "I already tried a weighted blanket" is the #1 barrier for Bearaby's best prospects -- people who want better sleep and have already spent money trying to solve it. Opening with "I'd tried three" positions the creator as a peer who's been burned before, not a naive convert. The UX designer credibility marker addresses the "thorough researcher" persona documented in the brand doc (customers describe themselves as people who "do their homework" before buying). The oversized sweatshirt and no-ring-light setup signals authenticity without a brand disclaimer -- it looks like organic content from someone who just wanted to tell their friends, not a paid placement.

Why this works:
- Cites specific brand doc content (hesitation list, #1 barrier)
- Explains WHY each creative choice serves the audience, not just that it does
- References the customer persona archetype ("thorough researcher") directly from brand doc language
- Gives QA enough to verify the claim is grounded

## The QA Layer: The 150-Point Rubric

The brief doesn't ship until it passes QA. Goodo runs a second agent explicitly after every brief generation. Here's the rubric that agent uses.

### Scoring Structure

10 criteria per concept × 5 points each = 50 points per concept × 3 concepts = 150 points total.

**Verdict thresholds:**
- 40-50 per concept: Ready to Produce
- 25-39 per concept: Revise and Resubmit
- Below 25 per concept: Needs Major Rework (flag for Matthew)

**The verdict = the lowest-scoring concept.** One weak concept fails the entire brief.

### Hard Gate (Runs Before Scoring)

**Brand Intelligence Verification:** Does the brief reference specific facts from the brand doc?

If no: mark ALL criteria 1/5, return "FAIL -- brand intelligence doc not loaded." Don't score. Don't revise. Go back to Step 1.

**Product Value Depth:** After reading this brief, would a cold viewer understand what this product actually does, why it's valuable, and why they should care deeply enough to take action?

Four sub-checks:
1. **Setup vs. sell ratio** -- Count lines on creative scenario vs. lines on product value. Heavily skewed toward setup = immediate flag.
2. **Mechanism present** -- Not just WHAT the product does, but HOW it works differently.
3. **Back half sells** -- The brief must deepen value after the hook, not trail off.
4. **"Would I Switch?" test** -- Would someone currently using a competitor feel motivated to switch?

### Video Ad Criteria (10 × 5 points)

**1. Hook (0-3s)**
5 = Visual + audio + text all present; strong pattern interrupt; open loop created; impossible to describe as "generic"
4 = All three present; pattern interrupt works; could be more specific
3 = Two of three present; pattern interrupt is weak
2 = Only one dimension present; no clear pattern interrupt
1 = Hook is a description of a hook, not an actual hook

**2. Script & Copy Quality**
5 = Named framework (PAS/AIDA/BAB) used correctly; "So What?" chain completed to emotional payoff; bucket brigades at transition points
4 = Framework used; chain partially completed
3 = Framework implied but not named; chain stops at benefit level
2 = No structural framework; claims stay at feature level
1 = Filler content; no persuasion structure

**3. Audience & Angle**
5 = Clear angle from the 8 generators; passes Angle Test (4+/5); awareness stage matched with justification from brand doc
4 = Angle named; awareness stage correct; justification thin
3 = Angle implied but not named; awareness stage plausible
2 = No clear angle; awareness stage assumed
1 = Could be for any brand's audience

**4. Structure & Pacing**
5 = Slippery Slide principle followed; product at midpoint; bucket brigades used; no dead spots
4 = Good structure; one pacing gap
3 = Structure exists; product arrives late or dead spots present
2 = Loose structure; multiple pacing problems
1 = No structural discipline

**5. CTA**
5 = Triple Stack: Benefit + Social Proof + Risk Reversal; specific to this product

## The 19 Mistakes Catalog

The QA system tracks recurring failures. These are the 19 patterns most often caught in real briefs.

### Hooks (6 Mistakes)

**Mistake 1: Generic hook that could be any brand's ad**
A hook that works for Bearaby, Purple, and every other weighted blanket is not a hook -- it's a category statement. The hook must be rooted in something only THIS brand can truthfully say or show.

**Mistake 2: Too long to get to the point**
Scroll-stop must happen in seconds 1-3. Any setup, context, or explanation that appears before the pattern interrupt is costing you viewers. Cut everything that isn't the hook.

**Mistake 3: Missing text hook for sound-off viewing**
85% of viewers scroll with sound off. If your hook only works with audio, 85% of your audience never heard it. Text hook is not optional.

**Mistake 4: Hook is a flat statement, not an open loop**
"This blanket changed my sleep" is a statement. It closes the loop. "Weighted blankets are supposed to help you sleep" -- then the blanket hits the floor -- is an open loop. It creates a question the viewer has to keep watching to answer.

**Mistake 5: Hook promises something the brief doesn't deliver**
If the hook creates an expectation, the body of the brief must fulfill it. "I can't believe this actually worked" followed by three benefits from the product page has broken a promise.

**Mistake 6: The hook is just boring**
No rubric saves a boring hook. If you read it and feel nothing, rewrite it before running the QA.

### Copy (6 Mistakes)

**Mistake 7: Brief barely explains product value (setup heavy, sell thin)**
Count the lines of creative scenario vs. lines of product explanation. If the concept spends 15 seconds on atmosphere and 5 seconds on why the product works, it's inverted. The audience came for the product, not the vibe.

**Mistake 8: No emotional payoff -- stops at benefit level**
"You'll sleep better" is a benefit. "You'll stop dreading 2am" is an emotional payoff. The "So What?" chain exists to get from one to the other. Stop running the chain before you reach emotion.

**Mistake 9: Wasted words and filler sentences**
In polished video: "okay," "honestly," "like," "literally," "so," "I mean." In static: "In today's fast-paced world," "Introducing," "We are proud to." Every word costs money at the production stage and attention at the viewing stage.

**Mistake 10: Too conversational, not enough sell**
UGC can be natural. But "natural" is not a substitute for persuasion. The authenticity paradox: it must feel real AND it must sell. If it only feels real, it's content. If it only sells, it's an ad. It needs to be both.

**Mistake 11: Benefits stay generic**
"Save time" is not a benefit for an ad. "Save 4 hours per week" is. "Improve your skin" is not a benefit. "Reduce redness in 72 hours, visible in photos" is. Every benefit needs a number, timeframe, or comparison.

**Mistake 12: No proof or credibility**
Claims without evidence are assertions. In ads, assertions get scrolled past. Proof stops scroll. If your brand doc has stats, quotes, or results, they belong in the brief -- not on a strategy deck somewhere.

### Audience (3 Mistakes)

**Mistake 13: No angle differentiation**
Eight positioning angles exist precisely because "this product is good" is not a message. An angle is what makes the message specific enough to reach one person. Briefs without named angles produce ads that feel vague to everyone.

**Mistake 14: Wrong awareness stage for the audience**
Running a Most-Aware ad to a cold audience is like proposing marriage on the first date. Running an Unaware ad to a retargeting audience is explaining a problem they already know they have. Stage must match audience.

**Mistake 15: Too broad, trying to speak to everyone**
An ad that speaks to everyone lands with no one. The brief must choose. If the target persona is "women 25-45 interested in wellness," you haven't chosen yet.

### Structure (4 Mistakes)

**Mistake 16: Dead spots in video briefs**
Every second of a 30-second spot costs money. A dead spot is any moment where nothing is happening to keep the viewer watching. They don't get a break in the middle of the ad -- that's when they scroll.

**Mistake 17: CTA disconnected from the rest**
The CTA should feel like the inevitable conclusion of everything that came before it. If someone reads the brief and the CTA feels like a different ad showed up at the end, the structure is broken.

**Mistake 18: UGC briefs don't feel native to platform**
The strongest signal that a UGC brief was written by someone who doesn't use social media: it doesn't feel like organic content. Real UGC starts in the middle of something. It doesn't introduce the product. It doesn't have a clear beginning.

**Mistake 19: No framework discipline**
Named frameworks exist because they work. "I used PAS structure here" means something. "Good structure" means nothing. Every structural choice should be nameable. If you can't name it, you can't QA it.

## Implementation Guide: How to Start Using This Tomorrow

### Day 1: Brand Doc Audit

Before you write a single brief, audit your brand docs against the 5 required elements.

For each client:
- Does the hesitation list have at least 3 specific objections (not "price" or "effectiveness")?
- Is there at least one proof point with a number?
- Is the product mechanism explained, not just the claim?
- Is there a "what this product is NOT" section?
- Is there a customer persona specific enough to write dialogue around?

Flag gaps. Fill them before brief generation starts.

If you don't have a brand doc at all: start with the full research prompt in Section 5 of the Brand Intelligence Deep Dive skill. Get at least the 5 elements populated before generating any brief.

### Day 2: First Brief

Pick one client. Pull up their brand doc. Run through the reference load order above.

Write the three concepts in the exact template format. Don't shortcut the "Why This Works" section -- this is where you prove to yourself that the brief is grounded, not intuited.

Then run the QA rubric yourself on your own brief. Score each criterion honestly. A score you inflated is a production problem you deferred.

### Week 1: Build the Reference Habit

The biggest shift in adopting this system is loading references BEFORE writing, not after. Most strategists generate the concept, then look for a framework to justify it. The Goodo system inverts this: load the frameworks first, then let the concept emerge from the intersection of framework + brand intelligence.

This feels slower at first. It isn't. It eliminates the revision cycle that happens when production realizes mid-shoot that the concept wasn't grounded in anything.

### Week 2: Run Two-Agent QA

If you're working with a team or a second strategist, implement the producer-verifier pattern. The person who wrote the brief cannot QA their own brief. The QA agent's job is to score honestly, not charitably.

If you're working solo: wait 24 hours after writing before QA. Fresh eyes catch what familiar ones miss.

### Week 3: Track Failure Patterns

Which of the 19 mistakes do you hit most often? Track them. A strategist who hits Mistake 7 (setup heavy, sell thin) on 60% of briefs needs to fix one specific thing, not everything.

## QA Self-Assessment Checklist

Run this before every brief ships.

### Brand Foundation
- [ ] All 5 brand doc elements verified (persona, hesitations, proof point, mechanism, what it's NOT)
- [ ] Every claim in the brief traces to a specific brand doc fact
- [ ] No concept contains generic claims that could apply to any competitor
- [ ] Brand doc loaded as Load 1 before any other reference

### Hooks
- [ ] Visual hook present for each concept (exact description, not "visual hook")
- [ ] Audio hook present (exact words, not "dialogue about the product")
- [ ] Text hook present (exact on-screen text, not "text reinforces the message")
- [ ] Each hook uses a named formula from direct-response-frameworks.md
- [ ] Each hook creates an open loop or pattern interrupt -- not a flat statement
- [ ] No hook is a description of what a hook should feel like

### Concepts
- [ ] Awareness stage named and justified with brand doc reference
- [ ] Positioning angle named and Angle Test passed (4+/5)
- [ ] Deep metaphor / Zaltman reference point identified
- [ ] Persuasion mechanism named (not implied)
- [ ] "Why This Works" cites specific brand doc facts (not truisms about authenticity or relatability)

### Video Ad Specific
- [ ] Beat-by-beat structure with actual dialogue (not "product enters," but the exact words)
- [ ] Product present by midpoint (by 15 seconds in a 30-second spot)
- [ ] Bucket brigade at at least one transition point
- [ ] Triple Stack CTA: Benefit + Social Proof + Risk Reversal
- [ ] No filler words in polished script (okay, honestly, like, literally)

### UGC/Creator Specific
- [ ] Creator profile: age range, setting, wardrobe, energy, credibility marker -- specific enough that casting can find this person
- [ ] Presenter archetype named (one of 4 from creator-ugc-guide.md)
- [ ] Conversational framework named
- [ ] Opening line: exact natural speech, no greeting, no brand intro
- [ ] 4 talking points in order, each citing brand doc
- [ ] Point 3 describes what creator PHYSICALLY SHOWS OR DOES (not just says)

### Static Ad Specific
- [ ] Headline uses Master Formula (Action + Outcome + Timeframe/Contrast)
- [ ] Headline pattern named
- [ ] Body copy under 125 characters
- [ ] At least one quantified benefit (number, timeframe, comparison)
- [ ] Visual direction complete: composition, subject, lighting, palette, text placement
- [ ] No AI copy tells (comprehensive, robust, leverage, game-changer, delve)

### Structure
- [ ] Exactly 3 concepts -- no more, no fewer
- [ ] Concept 1 = Video, Concept 2 = UGC, Concept 3 = Static
- [ ] Awareness stage distribution correct (cold/warm/hot)
- [ ] No strategic preamble, competitor analysis, media buying suggestions, or performance predictions
- [ ] File name follows convention: `[DATE]-[CLIENT_SLUG].md`

### QA Score
- [ ] Each concept scored individually (50 points max each)
- [ ] Verdict based on lowest-scoring concept
- [ ] If any concept scores below 25, flagged before production
- [ ] If brand intelligence hard gate fails, brief returned to Step 1 (not revised)

## One Last Thing

The system is only as good as the brand intelligence feeding it. A brief that follows every format rule perfectly but builds on vague, placeholder brand intelligence will still produce generic creative.

The hard gate isn't bureaucracy. It's the thing that separates creative strategy from creative decoration.

When you enforce it -- when you actually stop and say "I can't proceed until I know the three specific objections this audience has, not the category-level ones" -- you're doing the work that most agencies skip.

The briefs that come out the other side don't just look better. They direct better, shoot better, and perform better.

That's the system.
