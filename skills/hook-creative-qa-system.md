# The Hook & Creative QA System
## How Goodo Studios Writes Hooks That Stop Scroll -- and Catches What Doesn't Before Production

**Version 2.0** | The Creative Record | March 2026

---

## Introduction: Where the Work Lives or Dies

The first 3 seconds of a video ad or the headline of a static ad is where every creative either earns its budget or wastes it.

Not the middle of the video. Not the CTA. Not the product demo. The hook. Before the viewer knows what they're watching, before they understand what you're selling, before they have any reason to care -- the hook has already won or lost.

This sounds obvious. The fact that most creative hooks are bad suggests it isn't.

Bad hooks share a common characteristic: they describe what a hook should feel like instead of being one. "Attention-grabbing opening" is a brief note that a hook needs to exist. "Weighted blankets are supposed to help you sleep" -- said over footage of feet kicking a blanket off a bed at 2am -- is a hook.

The difference between these two things is the difference between a brief that gets produced and one that gets reshot.

This skill documents two things: the Goodo hook writing system -- the formulas, the angles, the structures, the tests -- and the QA layer that catches failures before they become expensive.

## The Structure of a Hook

Every hook in a Goodo brief has three dimensions. Best practice: all three are present.

### Dimension 1: Visual Hook

What does the viewer literally see in the first 3 seconds?

Not "a product shot." Not "a lifestyle scene." The exact, specific visual:

- "Extreme close-up of bare feet kicking a heavy blanket off a bed at 2am"
- "Overhead shot of a phone with 47 unread messages, all from the same thread"
- "Side-by-side: a wilted plant on a windowsill on Monday, same plant full and upright on Friday"

The visual hook is for everyone. It's the first thing the viewer processes, before they hear anything and before they read anything. It has to stop the scroll on its own.

Ask: would someone scrolling fast, looking for literally anything to do with their thumb, pause on this image?

If the answer is "maybe" or "it's interesting," that's not good enough. The answer needs to be "yes, and they'd be confused in a way that makes them want to stay."

### Dimension 2: Audio Hook

What are the exact words spoken or sounds heard in the first 3 seconds?

Not "voiceover about the problem." Not "creator introduces themselves." The exact words:

- "Weighted blankets are supposed to help you sleep." (Flat delivery. Then the blanket hits the floor.)
- "I've been doing this wrong for three years."
- "Okay, I was not going to post about this."

The audio hook does three things simultaneously:
1. Creates or deepens an open loop the viewer feels compelled to close
2. Signals the emotional register (the tone tells them whether this is relatable, revelatory, or provocative)
3. Filters for the right audience (specific language pre-qualifies; generic language tries to speak to everyone and reaches no one)

### Dimension 3: Text Hook

What text appears on screen in the first 3 seconds?

This is non-negotiable. 85% of viewers scroll with sound off. If your hook only works with audio, 85% of your potential audience never heard it.

The text hook must deliver the essential message independently. It's not a caption for the visual. It's a stand-alone message that creates the same open loop the audio creates for people with sound on.

Examples:
- "Why do I keep overheating?" (creates the open loop without audio)
- "3 weighted blankets. All in the closet by March." (delivers the before-state that makes the viewer ask what changed)
- "I stopped waking up at 2am" (delivers the result, creates curiosity about the method)

The text hook and audio hook don't need to say the same thing. They're speaking to different audiences (sound-on vs. sound-off) who should both feel the same pull to keep watching.

## The 7 Master Hook Formulas

Every hook in a Goodo brief uses a named formula. Named formulas serve two purposes: they enforce structural discipline at the writing stage, and they make QA possible -- you can't audit "good hook" but you can audit "Contrarian Take executed correctly."

### Formula 1: How-To Without Sacrifice

**Template:** How to [outcome] without [common sacrifice]

**When to use:** The audience wants the result but fears the typical cost -- time, money, effort, something they enjoy, something they'd have to give up.

**Why it works:** Pre-qualifies the ideal customer (the person who wants the outcome) while immediately removing the primary barrier (the sacrifice they assume is required).

**Examples:**
- "How to fall asleep in under 20 minutes without melatonin"
- "How to build lean muscle without spending more than 45 minutes in the gym"
- "How to eat the food you actually like without tracking every calorie"

**Text hook version:** "Sleep in 20 minutes. Zero melatonin."

**Common mistake:** The sacrifice has to be the real one -- the thing they actually fear, not the generic version. "Without giving up caffeine" only works if the audience actually believes that's the trade-off. "Without changing your entire morning routine" works if that's what they've tried and failed before.

---

### Formula 2: Number + Specificity

**Template:** [Number] [things] that [outcome] [timeframe]

**When to use:** The content that follows is genuinely actionable and scannable. Signals education, not a hard sell.

**Why it works:** Numbers create pattern interrupts (our brains process specific numbers faster than vague language). Specificity signals there's real content coming, not a brand statement.

**Examples:**
- "3 things I changed that helped me sleep through the night"
- "7 reasons weighted blankets aren't working for most people"
- "The 2-minute change that fixed my sleep in 4 days"

**Text hook version:** "3 things. 4 days. Actually slept."

**Common mistake:** The number needs to be real. "10 ways to sleep better" promises a listicle. If the ad only delivers one real thing, the number was a lie. Use the actual number of things you're going to cover.

---

### Formula 3: Before/After Gap

**Template:** From [specific bad state] to [specific good state] in [timeframe]

**When to use:** When the transformation is dramatic and visible, and the before-state is something the target audience experiences specifically.

**Why it works:** The gap creates immediate emotional connection (the before-state resonates) and immediate aspiration (the after-state is achievable). The timeframe makes it feel real rather than theoretical.

**Examples:**
- "From waking up at 2am every night to sleeping through until 7 -- in 2 weeks"
- "From avoiding mirrors to wearing a swimsuit for the first time in 8 years"
- "From 47 browser tabs to a clear head by noon"

**Text hook version:** "2 weeks. Sleeping through the night."

**Common mistake:** Both states need to be specific. "From stressed to calm" is not a Before/After Gap -- it's a brand promise. "From lying awake cataloguing tomorrow's problems to asleep by 10:30" is a Before/After Gap.

---

### Formula 4: Contrarian Take

**Template:** [Common widely-held belief] + evidence that challenges it

**When to use:** When there's a genuine misconception in the category that the target audience holds, and the product's value proposition is tied to correcting it.

**Why it works:** Challenges the audience's existing frame of reference. Creates cognitive dissonance that demands resolution. Works especially well when the belief is something the viewer has personally experienced as true.

**Examples:**
- Visual: feet kicking blanket off bed / Audio: "Weighted blankets are supposed to help you sleep." / Text: "Why do I keep overheating?"
- "Everyone told me to cut carbs. I kept gaining weight."
- "The harder I worked at the gym, the worse my results got."

**Text hook version:** The text hook should reinforce the challenge, not restate the belief. "Why you're overheating" not "Weighted blankets are supposed to help you sleep."

**Common mistake:** The contrarian must be genuinely true and challengeable. "You've been told X, but actually Y" only works if Y is genuinely better information, not just a different framing of the same thing. If the viewer tests the contrarian and it doesn't hold up, you've lost them.

---

### Formula 5: Curiosity Gap

**Template:** [Intriguing partial statement] -- [unexpected completion that creates more questions]

**When to use:** When there's a genuinely surprising insight, result, or mechanism behind the product that the audience wouldn't expect.

**Why it works:** Exploits the brain's native discomfort with incomplete information. Once the loop is open, the viewer can't close it without watching further.

**Examples:**
- "The reason your weighted blanket isn't working has nothing to do with weight"
- "I found out why I couldn't sleep -- and it wasn't what any doctor told me"
- "This thing stopped my 3am wake-ups and I still don't fully understand why"

**Text hook version:** "Not what you think." (Followed immediately by the surprising element.)

**Common mistake:** The gap needs to pay off. If the viewer keeps watching and the completion of the loop is "and then I bought a thing," they feel cheated. The curiosity gap must open a loop that the ad closes with real, satisfying content.

---

### Formula 6: Specific Person + Problem

**Template:** If you're [specific description of a person] struggling with [specific problem], this is for you.

**When to use:** Pre-qualification. When the audience is defined enough that specificity will feel like recognition, not exclusion.

**Why it works:** Hyper-specific address makes the target audience feel seen. The more specific the person description, the stronger the "that's me" recognition. People who don't match feel correctly filtered out (they weren't going to buy anyway).

**Examples:**
- "If you've tried every sleep trick on the internet and you're still lying awake at 3am..."
- "If you're a mom who hasn't slept through the night since your second kid was born..."
- "If you're the type of person who does their research before buying anything -- I need you to watch this"

**Text hook version:** "For people who've tried everything." (Filters immediately for the exhausted-searcher persona.)

**Common mistake:** The specificity has to match the actual customer persona from the brand doc. "If you're a busy professional" is not specific -- it's a category. "If you're the person who reads the Amazon reviews before buying a $12 phone charger" is specific.

---

### Formula 7: "I Wish I Knew"

**Template:** I wish someone had told me [counterintuitive truth] sooner.

**When to use:** When there's genuine insight the audience doesn't have that would have changed their behavior. Works especially well for products that solve problems people have accepted as unsolvable.

**Why it works:** Combines the authority of hindsight with the empathy of shared experience. The creator is positioned as someone who has the knowledge the viewer is missing.

**Examples:**
- "I wish someone had told me the problem wasn't the blanket -- it was the fill."
- "I wish I'd known that sleep latency and sleep quality are different problems."
- "I wish someone had told me that waking up tired after 8 hours is a solvable problem."

**Text hook version:** "I wish I'd known this sooner." (Simple. Effective. Makes viewers think they're about to learn something valuable.)

**Common mistake:** The "I wish I knew" must be genuinely valuable information, not a product pitch disguised as wisdom. "I wish I'd known about Bearaby sooner" is a sales message. "I wish I'd known the overheating problem has a different solution than a lighter blanket" is a real insight.

## The 8 Positioning Angles

Every hook is built on a positioning angle. The angle determines what makes the message specific enough to reach one person instead of trying to reach everyone.

### The 8 Angles

**1. Contrarian**
What does everyone believe that's wrong? What conventional wisdom, when challenged, reveals the product's unique value?
Risk: Can feel clickbaity if the contrarian isn't substantiated. The challenge must hold up.

**2. Unique Mechanism**
What's the HOW that makes the promise believable? Not the outcome -- the specific mechanism that produces it.
Risk: Can feel gimmicky if the mechanism isn't genuinely unique or if it's described in marketing language instead of real terms.

**3. Transformation**
What's the before/after gap? What identity or state does the product enable the customer to move into?
Risk: Overdone. Requires specificity to feel real rather than like every other before/after ad.

**4. Enemy**
What common frustration, experience, or villain can you position against? What does the audience hate that the product eliminates?
Risk: Can feel negative or fear-based. Balance with the solution, not just the enemy.

**5. Speed/Ease**
How does this product compress time or reduce effort? What would take longer or cost more effort without it?
Risk: If you can't deliver on speed/ease, this angle backfires badly. Only use if the claim is real.

**6. Specificity**
Who exactly is this for and what exactly does it solve? The angle is the precision of the audience definition.
Risk: Too narrow excludes viable customers. The balance is specificity within a large enough segment.

**7. Social Proof**
What evidence can lead the message? Real numbers, real names, real results that prove the claim.
Risk: Numbers without context feel hollow. "50,000 happy customers" means nothing. "50,000 people who say they finally sleep through the night" means something.

**8. Risk Reversal**
What guarantee or trial removes the reason not to act? Makes the primary barrier (fear of wasting money) disappear.
Risk: Only works if you can actually deliver. Risk reversal on a product that frequently fails its guarantee destroys trust.

### The Angle Test (Required Before Finalizing)

Score 1 point for each yes:

1. Is it true? Can you back this up with evidence from the brand doc?
2. Is it relevant? Does this matter to your specific audience -- not an audience in general?
3. Is it different? Is this genuinely distinct from what competitors are saying?
4. Is it provable? Can you show evidence, not just claim it?
5. Is it ownable? Is this angle harder for competitors to copy than a generic benefit claim?

**Score 4+/5:** Strong angle. Proceed.
**Score 3/5:** Refine before using. Which of the 5 questions failed? Fix that.
**Score below 3/5:** Find a different angle.

## Hook Writing by Awareness Stage

The formula and angle don't exist in isolation -- they must serve the awareness stage the concept is targeting. A hook that would work beautifully for a Problem-Aware audience will confuse an Unaware audience and bore a Product-Aware one.

### Hooks for Unaware Audiences

The audience doesn't know they have a problem. The hook has to create recognition of a situation before it can create recognition of a problem.

**What works:**
- Mirror their experience precisely before naming the problem ("You lie awake. Not anxious, not worried, just... awake.")
- The "wait, that's me" visual hook (footage of an experience they recognize)
- Specific Person formula with extremely high specificity
- "I Wish I Knew" about a common experience everyone has had

**What doesn't work:**
- Starting with the product or category
- Naming the problem before demonstrating it
- Contrarian Take (they have to hold the belief first)

**The principle:** Lead with their life, not your product. The hook is recognizing them, not introducing yourself.

### Hooks for Problem-Aware Audiences

The audience knows the problem. They're in it. They've maybe tried to solve it and failed. The hook must validate and amplify the specific experience.

**What works:**
- Contrarian Take (challenges the conventional solutions they've already tried)
- Before/After Gap (names the before-state with precision, implies the after is real)
- Specific Person + Problem (hits their exact experience)
- "I Wish I Knew" about a problem they've accepted as unsolvable

**What doesn't work:**
- Explaining the problem (they already know)
- Generic problem statements ("tired of bad sleep?" -- yes, obviously)
- Leading with the product before validating the problem

**The principle:** Validate and amplify. The audience doesn't need to be told they have a problem -- they need to feel that you understand exactly what their version of the problem is.

### Hooks for Solution-Aware Audiences

The audience knows solutions exist and has probably tried some. The hook must differentiate this solution from what they've already tried and create reason to believe.

**What works:**
- Unique Mechanism (HOW this works differently)
- Contrarian Take on the solutions they've tried (not the problem)
- Number + Specificity (specific number of things they haven't tried yet)
- Social Proof (evidence from people who were also skeptics)

**What doesn't work:**
- Hooks that explain the problem (they know)
- Hooks that introduce the category (they know)
- Generic transformation hooks (they've heard before/after before)

**The principle:** Lead with unique mechanism. Why THIS solution when they've already tried others?

### Hooks for Product-Aware Audiences

The audience knows the product. They've probably been to the website. Something stopped them. The hook must address the last objection.

**What works:**
- Social Proof with specifics that address the doubt
- Risk Reversal (guarantee, trial, no-risk)
- Transformation hook that shows someone with the same hesitation who bought and was glad
- Before/After Gap with someone who almost didn't buy

**What doesn't work:**
- Category-level hooks (they're past this)
- Explaining the product mechanism (they know)
- Introducing anything new (this is the close, not the open)

**The principle:** Proof. Specifics. Removing the last objection. The hook leads with evidence, not education.

### Hooks for Most-Aware Audiences

The audience wants the product. Give them a reason to act NOW.

**What works:**
- Offer, urgency, or new news ("free shipping this week," "new color just dropped")
- Short Social Proof that validates the decision they're already making
- Direct, specific, benefit-leading

**What doesn't work:**
- Anything that introduces friction or new information
- Re-explaining the product
- Emotional hooks designed for colder audiences (they're past that)

**The principle:** Direct offer. Short. Specific. No warm-up needed.

## The Two-Agent QA Layer

Goodo runs two agents on every creative brief. The first agent writes. The second agent QAs. The same person cannot do both -- familiarity with the work creates blind spots that make good-faith scores feel like 4/5 when they're actually 2/5.

### What the QA Agent Looks For

**Hard Gate 1: Brand Intelligence Verification**
Does the brief reference specific facts from the brand doc? If no: mark ALL criteria 1/5, return "FAIL -- brand intelligence doc not loaded." No scoring, no revision notes -- go back and load the brand doc.

**Hard Gate 2: Product Value Depth**
Ask: After reading this brief, would a cold viewer understand what this product actually does, why it's valuable, and why they should care deeply enough to take action?

Four sub-checks:
1. **Setup vs. sell ratio.** Count lines of creative scenario vs. lines of product value. Heavily skewed toward setup = flag.
2. **Mechanism present.** Not just WHAT, but HOW it works differently.
3. **Back half sells.** After the hook delivers, does the brief deepen the value or trail off?
4. **"Would I Switch?" test.** Would someone currently using a competitor feel motivated to switch?

### Hook-Specific QA Criteria

When reviewing a hook, the QA agent works within the creative concept, not against it. The job is to strengthen the execution of the concept, not replace the concept.

**Hook scoring (5 points):**
5 = Visual + audio + text all present; strong pattern interrupt; clear open loop; impossible to describe as generic; passes Mute Test (sound off = full message delivered)
4 = All three present; pattern interrupt works; could be more specific to the brand or audience
3 = Two of three dimensions present; pattern interrupt is weak or generic
2 = Only one dimension present; no pattern interrupt; viewer has no reason to keep watching
1 = Hook is a description of a hook ("attention-grabbing opening that shows the contrast between")

**The hook feedback protocol:**
When QA suggests hook revisions:
- Provide 2-3 alternatives within the same creative concept (different executions, same direction)
- Label any significantly different approach clearly as "ALTERNATIVE DIRECTION" so it's clearly a departure
- Never simply say "make this more engaging" -- write the actual alternative

### The "So What?" Chain

Every claim in a brief must complete the "So What?" chain. The chain runs from feature to emotional payoff:

```
Feature: What the product is
↓
Functional: What it does
↓
Financial: What it saves or earns
↓
Emotional: How it feels
```

**Bearaby 30-day trial example:**
- Feature: 30-day return policy
- Functional: Try it risk-free
- Financial: Don't waste $200 on something that doesn't work
- Emotional: Feel confident ordering without the fear of regret
- Headline: "Order with confidence -- love it or get every penny back"

The QA agent checks where the chain stops. A brief that delivers the functional benefit but stops before emotional payoff is a 3/5. A brief that delivers all four stages with specificity is a 5/5.

**Practicing the chain:**
Take any feature from your brand doc. Run it through all four stages. Keep asking "so what?" until you're at an emotion so specific and real that you could pick it out in a lineup of human feelings.

"Sleep better" -- so what? "Wake up feeling rested instead of groggy" -- so what? "Walk into work not counting the hours until you can leave" -- so what? "Feel like yourself again instead of a person running on borrowed energy."

That last one is the hook.

### Universal Copy Standards

These apply across all formats. Zero tolerance.

**Never:**
- Em dashes in any ad copy. Use commas or periods. Em dashes are a literary device; ads are spoken or scanned.
- "Comprehensive," "robust," "leverage," "game-changer," "in today's fast-paced world," "delve," "transformative"
- "I'm so excited to share," "You won't believe," "Don't miss out on," "This product is amazing because"
- Benefits stated without specificity ("save time," "improve results," "feel better")

**Always:**
- Specificity over generality. 4 hours/week over "time savings."
- Customer language from reviews, not marketing language invented at the desk.
- Text hook in every video and UGC concept.
- Named framework for every structural choice.

## The Full Concept Checklist

Run this before ANY concept is finalized.

### Foundation
- [ ] Awareness stage named and justified (not assumed -- tied to specific brand doc evidence)
- [ ] Deep metaphor / Zaltman reference point identified
- [ ] Positioning angle named and Angle Test passed (4+/5)
- [ ] Hook written in the language of the identified metaphor

### Hook
- [ ] Visual hook: exact description of what viewer sees
- [ ] Audio hook: exact dialogue or sound (for video/UGC)
- [ ] Text hook: exact on-screen text
- [ ] Hook formula named (one of 7)
- [ ] Hook is literal words/visuals -- not a description of what it should feel like
- [ ] Open loop or pattern interrupt present -- not a flat statement
- [ ] Mute test passed (text hook delivers message independently)

### Copy and Structure
- [ ] "So What?" chain completed to emotional payoff
- [ ] No benefits stopped at functional level
- [ ] Structure framework named (PAS, AIDA, BAB, etc.)
- [ ] Bucket brigades at transition points (where energy might drop)
- [ ] CTA matches awareness stage (Unaware = curiosity; Most-Aware = urgency)

### Brand Grounding
- [ ] Every claim traces to brand doc
- [ ] No generic claims applicable to any competitor
- [ ] Product mechanism present (HOW it works, not just what it claims)
- [ ] Customer language from reviews used where possible
- [ ] "What this product is NOT" respected -- no forbidden comparisons

### Format Compliance
- [ ] Video: Beat structure present (0-3s, 3-8s, 8-15s, 15-30s)
- [ ] Video: Product at midpoint
- [ ] Video: No filler words in polished script
- [ ] UGC: Creator profile specific (not a demographic)
- [ ] UGC: Presenter archetype named
- [ ] UGC: Opening line in natural speech (not "Hi guys!")
- [ ] UGC: Talking Point 3 describes physical action (what creator SHOWS, not just says)
- [ ] Static: Headline formula named
- [ ] Static: Under 125 characters body copy
- [ ] Static: Visual direction complete (composition, subject, lighting, palette, text placement)

### Copy Standards
- [ ] No em dashes
- [ ] No AI copy tells
- [ ] Benefits quantified (numbers, timeframes, comparisons)
- [ ] No generic praise language

## Common Hook Mistakes and How to Fix Them

### Mistake: The Description Hook

**Bad:** "Attention-grabbing visual that shows the contrast between poor and great sleep."

**Why it fails:** The brief writer knows what they want to happen but hasn't done the work of figuring out how to make it happen. This creates a gap that only gets filled at production -- where it costs the most to fix.

**Fix:** Write the actual visual. Not what the hook should feel like -- what the viewer sees. Frame the shot. Name the subject. Specify the lighting if it matters. The exact image.

---

### Mistake: The Flat Statement Hook

**Bad:** "Our weighted blanket helps you sleep better every night."

**Why it fails:** A flat statement closes the loop. There's nothing to watch for. The viewer knows everything they need to know in 3 seconds and has no reason to stay.

**Fix:** Find the open loop in the brand doc. Which claim invites a "but how?" or creates a "wait, that's happening to me" moment? Build the hook around the moment before the answer arrives.

---

### Mistake: The Generic Before/After

**Bad:** "From tired to energized."

**Why it fails:** Every before/after ad ever made could use this. It doesn't resonate because it's not specific enough to trigger real recognition.

**Fix:** Name the specific moment of the before-state. "From waking up at 2am cataloguing tomorrow's to-do list" is specific. "From tired" is a category. If you can't be specific about the before-state, the brand doc doesn't have enough customer intelligence yet.

---

### Mistake: The Missing Text Hook

**Bad:** Hook has a strong visual and audio component but no on-screen text.

**Why it fails:** 85% of viewers scroll with sound off. This is not a hypothetical -- it's the default behavior on Instagram and TikTok. A hook that only works with audio is a hook that works for 15% of your audience.

**Fix:** Write the text hook first. Then build the audio hook to complement it. The text hook should be able to stand alone. The audio hook adds for viewers who have sound on.

---

### Mistake: The Promised-But-Undelivered Loop

**Bad:** Hook: "I can't believe this actually worked." Body: Three product benefit bullet points.

**Why it fails:** The hook created an expectation of a story -- "what worked? how did you find out? what happened?" -- and delivered a product feature list instead. The viewer was promised a narrative and got an ad.

**Fix:** If the hook creates a story expectation, the body of the brief must deliver a story. If you're going to use "I can't believe this worked," the body needs to show the arc: what they tried before, why they were skeptical, what happened when they used it, the specific result.

---

### Mistake: The Wrong Awareness Stage Hook

**Bad:** Running a Problem-Aware hook to a cold/Unaware audience.

**Example:** "Tired of tossing and turning all night?" to a cold audience that doesn't think they have a sleep problem.

**Why it fails:** The Unaware audience doesn't toss and turn -- they just don't sleep as well as they could. They haven't named the problem yet. A hook that names the problem assumes they're already further along the awareness spectrum than they are.

**Fix:** Map the awareness stage before writing the hook. For Unaware audiences, the hook mirrors their experience without naming the problem. "You lie awake. Not thinking about anything specific. Just... awake." That's an Unaware hook. They recognize themselves before they recognize that it's a problem.

## Implementation Guide: Building the Two-Agent QA Habit

### Step 1: Separate Writing from Reviewing

The simplest version of the two-agent model: finish writing the brief, close the document, open it the next day before reviewing. Distance creates perspective. Same work, different eyes.

If you have a partner: brief writer and reviewer are never the same person on the same brief.

### Step 2: Score Before You Fix

Run the 10-criteria scoring rubric before you start revising. This prevents the common habit of fixing the most obvious things first (which are often not the most important things).

Score all 10 criteria. Then sort by score. Fix the lowest scores first -- those are where the most production risk lives.

### Step 3: Run the "So What?" Chain Before QA

This is a separate check from the rubric and should happen before formal scoring. Read every claim in the brief and run the chain. Where does it stop? Any claim that stops at functional or financial level needs another iteration before QA scores it.

### Step 4: Write Alternative Hooks Before Approving

Even if the hook scores 4/5 in QA, write one alternative. This discipline produces better briefs over time -- not because the alternative is always better, but because the exercise of rewriting the hook from a different angle reveals whether the current hook is genuinely the best approach or just the first approach that cleared the bar.

### Step 5: Track Failure Patterns

After 10 briefs, look at your QA scores. Which of the 19 mistake patterns do you hit most often? Which criteria score lowest consistently?

A strategist who consistently underperforms on Product Value Depth (Mistake 7: setup heavy, sell thin) needs to fix one habit -- writing the setup before knowing how much product value they have to work with. That's a fixable problem with a specific solution.

Track the patterns. Fix the patterns. The rubric tells you where to look.
